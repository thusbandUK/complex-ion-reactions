Substitution reactions

These are ligand substitution reactions or ligand exchange reactions.

As the name suggests this is when one or more ligands in a complex are substituted for other ligands.

As you will see below, not all ligand exchange reactions involve a change in coordination number, so why does it happen in this case?

How do the size and charge of chloro ligands compare to aqua ligands? How might that affect the shape of the complex ion?

Changing shape

Chloro ligands are larger than water ligands, which may not be self-evident.

Although aqua ligands each contain three atoms, two of these are hydrogen atoms, which are very small. Meanwhile oxygen has two electron shells compared to three in each chloride ion.

Consequently there is only space for four chloro ligands around the central copper ion. Furthermore this arrangement minimises the repulsion between the negatively charged chloro ligands.

Cobalt and iron(III) also form tetrahedral complexes when reacted with excess hydrochloric acid.

How can we explain this?

These are hydrolysis reactions.

Looking at an equation…

[Cu(H2O)6]2+ + 2OH- → [Cu(H2O)4(OH)2] + 2H2O

It seems as if two hydroxide ions have each been swapped for a water molecule, which would make this a ligand exchange reaction, but actually, that’s not what’s happening.

So what is going on?

Hydrolysis Mechanism

Look at the mechanism. See how the yellow hydrogen atoms form bonds to the blue oxygen atoms from the attacking hydroxide ions? A hydrolysis reaction is when bonds are broken by water molecules or hydroxide ions. We can see that the hydroxide ions are breaking O-H bonds in the aqua ligands.

Did you notice the complexes produced...

  • ...both have enough hydroxide ion ligands to equalise charge of metal ions
  • ...both have no overall charge
  • ...have different numbers of hydroxide ion and water molecule ligands
  • ...have central metal ions with different oxidation states

So why are the precipitates insoluble?

Losing two H+ ions gives each of the precipitates a neutral overall charge. As a result they do not dissolve in water, since they are attracted neither to the partially positive hydrogen atoms in water, nor to the partially negative oxygen atoms.

Why does the aluminium hydroxide precipitate dissolve?

Aluminium hydroxide reacts with excess sodium hydroxide to produce a tetrahedral ion called aluminium tetrahydrate. Aluminium hydroxide can be represented as [Al(H2O)3(OH)3] or with the simplified formula Al(OH)3

Remember also that the hydrolysis reactions are reversible, meaning that aluminium hydroxide can react with excess acid to turn back into hexaaquaaluminium as follows...

[Al(H2O)3(OH)3] + 3H+ ⇌ [Al(H2O)6]3+

This shows the amphoteric nature of aluminium hydroxide, which means its ability to react with both acids and bases.

The reason why a tetrahedral complex is produced, rather than [Al(OH)6]3- is beyond the scope of the AQA course.

Why do these observations look so familiar?

These observations are identical to the observations recorded when a few drops of sodium hydroxide are added to the same solutions.

That is because the same reaction is occurring, although we can represent the process with various different equations.

Solutions of sodium hydroxide and ammonia are both alkaline, meaning they both release hydroxide ions in solution.

NH3 + H2O ⇌ NH4+ + OH–

In equations we can show the complex ions reacting directly with ammonia molecules, as follows:

[Fe(H2O)6]2+ + 2NH3 ⇌ [Fe(H2O)4(OH)2] + 2NH4+

Or we can show the complex ions reacting with hydroxide ions, provided we also include an equation to show how ammonia produces hydroxide ions in aqueous solution.

Ligand exchange

When ammonia is added to hexaaquacopper(II) in excess, a ligand exchange reaction occurs in which four aqua (water) ligands are exchanged for four ammonia molecules, which become ammine ligands.

Ligand exchange also occurs with hexaaquacobalt(II) but in this case, all six aqua ligands are substituted for ammine ligands, as shown in the equation.

[Co(H2O)6]2+ + 6NH3 ⇌ [Co(NH3)6]2+ + 6H2O

You will not be expected to explain why substition is complete for cobalt but not for copper. A proper explanation requires reference to something called crystal field theory which is beyond the scope of the A-level course. Questions in the exam will be limited to the complex ions listed in the specification.

How do these observations compare to the reactions with sodium hydroxide?

Notice that only two of the aqua ions, Fe(III) and Al(III), produce effervesence.

Also the precipitates produced by the cobalt and copper solutions are different colours to their precipitates with sodium hydroxide.

Meanwhile the precipitates produced by Fe(II), Fe(III) and Al(III) are the same colour with both sodium carbonate and sodium hydroxide.

How can we explain these observations?


If we ignore the iron(II) result for a moment, there is a clear pattern between the observations and the oxidation state of the central metal ion. The Co2+ and Cu2+ solutions produce different colour precipitates, whereas the Fe3+ and Al3+ solutions produce effervescence.

The metal(II) solutions form metal carbonate precipitates, eg:

Co2+ + CO32+ ⇌ CoCO3

But the metal(III) solutions have a lower pH (ie are more acidic) than the metal(II) solutions. Consequently they react with the carbonate ions to produce carbon dioxide gas. Since the metal(III) complex ions donate H+ ions for this acid base reaction, they produce the same metal hydroxide precipitates as with sodium hydroxide.

[Fe(H2O)6]3+ + 3/2CO32+ → [Fe(H2O)3(OH)3] + 3/2CO2 + 3/2H2O

Finally iron(II) produces a carbonate precipitate like copper and cobalt, but it just happens to be close enough in colour to its hydroxide precipitate that both are called green.

Why are M3+ solutions more acidic?

The Fe3+ ion has a smaller ionic radius than the Fe2+ ion, as well as a higher charge, meaning that it has a higher charge density. Consequently it exerts a stronger attraction on nearby electrons, including those in O—H bonds.

Notice that the O—H bond in the iron(III) complex is more polar than the O—H bond in the iron(II) complex, so not only is the Fe3+ ion more positive, but so are the H atoms in the aqua ligands. As a result there is stronger repulsion between the two, meaning that H+ ions are lost more easily from the iron(III) complex.

How to phrase it like the AQA mark scheme: hexaaqua metal(III) complexes are more acidic than hexaaqua metal(II) complexes because the greater charge density of the metal ion has a more strongly polarising effect on the O—H bonds. This weakens the O—H bonds, meaning that more H+ ions dissociate, which reduces the pH.
